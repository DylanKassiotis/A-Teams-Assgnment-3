Project Description


Overview


Topic

Our project idea involves a health website which provides practical, applicable strategies to improve one’s health using wisdom from a variety of traditional health systems such as Ayurveda, Traditional Chinese Medicine and modern Western science. The goal is to empower laypersons and middle-class workers to make better decisions with ideas and concepts tailored to their body profile, so we don’t resort to pharmaceutical medicine, doctors and the next fad diet to ‘magically fix’ basic health issues. The website would feature a series of tests, provide the user with a profile and also include a blog which would elaborate on the ideas presented in the profile. As time goes on, a neural network could be added, and this would increase the value of the site drastically. The website could also host practitioners who are interested in contributing to the project.

Motivation

In my personal experience, a lot of us (myself included) can fall out of touch with our bodies and live life making repeated mistakes in food choices. Nowadays it’s common to suffer from lupus, blood sugar imbalances, etc. – and most of the time it isn’t necessary. In other words, laxatives are a quick fix, but the root cause of one’s constipation is probably their own choices. Unfortunately, ancient, modern and naturopathic practitioners always seem to be at odds with one another because somebody’s livelihood is at stake, rather than seeing where they fall short and others shine. To a potential employer, this project would show website and java (test app) programming skills, web design skills and the ability to work as a team.

Landscape

Most websites deal with information sparsely as blog posts, or centre on one strategy (paleo, keto, etc.) and push it as something everybody should do, when statistics and data show that this is not at all wise. There are a few sites on Ayurveda, and the material on TCM is extremely limited. In short, there are no health websites currently that provide information from a variety of perspectives; most are limited to one school of thought without any kind of critical analysis.


Detailed Description

Aims

Our project’s aim is to create a health website that provides users with strategies based on a blend of ancient wisdom and modern naturopathic science. The main feature of the site is a test that returns a user-generated profile, and a health blog which elaborates on ideas from various schools of thought. The purpose of the site is to help people build confidence in their every-day decision making, optimize their health and achieve a greater level of balance in life.

Goals

Rent a Domain

In the beginning this will be a relatively small-scale project, so it makes sense to first rent a domain and upscale if the site gains a lot of traffic. There are many sites out there with which to register a domain, for example domain.com.

Create Blog Material, or Source a Practitioner

The website’s core or most valuable part will be the blog, as this is where the information is. A team member could attend a school or course, but ultimately practitioners (preferably qualified, to build audience confidence, ensure the integrity of the site and to bring real-life experience to the project) who like the project and are willing to contribute will have to be sourced. Each school of thought is specialized, so it’s not likely any one person will be able to write the blog themselves beyond a superficial level.

Hire a Graphic Designer/Web Developer

Simply coding a blog may be simple enough, but as the team adds more features to the website such as user accounts/profiles and a test, a higher level of skill will be required. If necessary, the team could post job advertisements on Jobseeker or Seek, hold interviews and find somebody looking for a short-term contract to set the site up. Preferably they would also have graphic design skills to create visuals to reinforce ideas in the blog.

Design a Test + User-Generated Profile

Here the project’s creative lead will consult the various practitioners involved in the site to devise formulas and parameters for a test and send these to the web developer, who will code them into an applet. The test will cover the basic somatotypes and cross-check answers to identify a predisposition to various ailments, then generate a profile containing various preventative strategies, either dietary or lifestyle-based (or both).

Internal Testing + Beta Testing

The team will either test the first few iterations themselves or hire a group of beta testers (this is discussed under ‘testing’). However, as no project is perfect on first release it seems wise to continue iterating on the test as a beta to receive user feedback on how the UI/interface could be improved, catch bugs, etc. When the team is confident the test operates properly and delivers results accurately, true to how these things would be seen in the field, then we will move on to the final release.



Plans and Progress

Health Blog

The health blog forms the core of the website and will be comprised mostly of lowerresolution information (i.e., body types, information to do with the senses, tastes, dryness vs. dampness in the body, which foods are considered tonics for certain organs, etc.) from Ayurveda and Traditional Chinese Medicine. This kind of information is the most readily applicable in everyday life and will produce the most immediate results and is what will attract most people to the website. To attain the information, a practitioner could be sourced, or one could do the research/attend a school themselves. The blogs will be drafted by a practitioner, sent to the team to be edited/screened, then delivered to the web developer. Which will code them into the website and create/add visuals as specified to the core structure and represented data and figures. The blog will be organised under several categories, such as:

These will be accessible either from a drop-down menu or a sidebar.

Design a Test

For the website to have a user-generated profile, it must also have a test that takes inputs. For test design, an experienced programmer would be hired and work together with one or more specialists. The test also encourages users to think about and identify various features of their body and if/how they fit together to form a picture or archetype. Test parameters would include typical indicators of body type and lifestyle such as BMI, waistto-hip ratio, fingers encircling the wrist, facial features, skin health, personal and family health history, etc. The way these inputs interact with one another determines the generated profile (I.e., high waist-to-hip ratio + cold, oily skin + circulatory issues --> ‘kapha’-type strategy & foods).


These inputs will be stored in the back end and called upon by an algorithm devised by the project lead and several practitioners when generating the user profile. For writing the test and coding the website the team will look for a programmer specialising in java or C++, as these are the most widely used and accessible languages. As java applets are no longer in fashion, it is more likely the test will be coded directly into the website.

Accounts & User-generated Profile

The site will host user accounts within which their test results & information will be stored. They will also be able to optionally enter their emails and receive updates on, for example, when the test gets a new feature, or an original blog post is added.

The user-generated profile will return the primary features of their body profile on the macro-level, as well as lower-resolution and higher-resolution strategies to maintain balance. Lower-resolution strategies are those that follow the advice of ancient wisdom and are quite generally applicable (e.g., incorporating the six tastes and which ones to emphasize, or dryness vs dampness in the body). Higher-resolution strategies are based on modern science, and may include the macro-aspects of a diet, the recommendation of specific herbs/supplements and certain practices and exercises (e.g., fasting and how to fast, exercise for cardiovascular health, meditation, etc.). The most important part of the website is the blog. However, the user-generated profile is where the user connects the dots between their inputs and recommended strategies and receives the most immediately relevant and useful information. They may also find recommendations they would have missed or otherwise not considered.

Testing

As our project is a website, there will be little testing required. Blog drafts will be screened and edited before they are uploaded; otherwise, most testing will occur in the test and profile design. Essentially, we will know the website works when the application and generated profile match the set of pre-devised parameters and formulas initially sent to the programmer. The bug hunting process could be made faster by delegating each team member with a certain subset of formulas to test, or by hiring a group of testers, though this would not likely be necessary as it would be light work between 5-6 people. Given that the formulas are provided, testers would only need basic intuitive knowledge of math and the internet. The website could also release a beta test for members who have signed up for the blog to test; this would be a more efficient but less comprehensive way of making sure the program works (more likely to catch random visual bugs, etc.) and the site operator could gather feedback from users to further improve the visuals, test format, site layout, etc. Success will mean delivering a well-iterated product that functions according to specifications; the inputs will match the outputs, the interface will be clean and intuitive, and the back end will relate information properly to the front-end without any holes, bugs or glitches.

Risks

Risks within this project are mostly to do with communication and integrity of vision. For example, there is always the risk a graphic designer may not share passion for the project and deliver a product lacking conceptual or ideological consistency, or a blog contributor may provide shallow or inaccurate information. Therefore, the screening process is important. To consider other aspects, important care should be taken when interviewing and selecting potential collaborators (I.e., only accept those with a portfolio, preferably of original ideas/projects); this should not be rushed.

In test design, there is also the risk a programmer will create an applet using software incompatible with the website, or that cannot be properly embedded, etc. If the team chooses to follow this route, it will be important to specify to the programmer a medium that can be easily integrated with the website.

Finally, as the site will hold sensitive information tied to user accounts it runs the risk of a security breach. In addition to standard measures (e.g., requiring passwords to include a variety of symbols), it would be wise to encrypt the data or place it behind other security measures. This could be included in the programmer’s job description, or a cyber security specialist could be separately hired.
